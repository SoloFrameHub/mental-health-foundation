How to Use Your Course Companion Notebook

Maximize your learning with these proven strategies

Daily Practice Routine

Set aside 10-15 minutes after each lesson to complete reflection prompts and practice exercises. Consistency matters more than perfection—even brief daily engagement produces better outcomes than sporadic intensive sessions.

Provider Tip: "In my practice, patients who use guided reflection after lessons show 40% better skill retention and application to real-life situations."
Follow the Guided Prompts

Each lesson includes specific reflection questions designed to deepen understanding and personalize concepts to your situation. These aren't busywork—they're evidence-based exercises proven to enhance learning and skill transfer.

Research Shows: Structured reflection increases therapy effectiveness by 60% compared to passive content consumption alone.
Review & Revise Regularly

Return to previous entries weekly to notice patterns, track progress, and refine your understanding. Your early reflections become valuable data showing how far you've come and what strategies work best for you.

Growth Insight: "When I look back at my first entries, I barely recognize that anxious person. Seeing my progress in black and white was transformational." - Course Graduate
Bring It to Therapy

If you're working with a therapist, export your notebook as PDF and share relevant entries. Your reflections provide rich material for therapy sessions and help your therapist understand your progress between appointments.

Therapist Feedback: "When clients bring structured reflections from courses, we can focus therapy time on personalization and deeper work rather than teaching basics."
Sample Reflection Prompts from Your Notebook
After Lesson 1: The Cognitive Triangle
  • Think of a recent situation that triggered strong emotions. What were your automatic thoughts in that moment?
  • How did those thoughts influence what you felt and what you did?
  • Looking back now, can you identify a different thought you could have had that might have changed your emotional response?
After Lesson 5: Cognitive Restructuring
  • Choose one anxious thought from this week. What evidence supports this thought? What evidence contradicts it?
  • What's a more balanced alternative thought that accounts for all the evidence?
  • How does this alternative thought change your emotional response and behavior?
Weekly Progress Check-In
  • What CBT skills did you practice this week? How did they work?
  • What was your biggest challenge? What helped you work through it?
  • What's one thing you learned about yourself this week that surprised you?
  • On a scale of 1-10, how confident do you feel using these techniques? What would increase that confidence?

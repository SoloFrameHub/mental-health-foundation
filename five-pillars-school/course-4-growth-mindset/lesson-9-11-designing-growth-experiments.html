üß™ The HYPOTHESIS Framework

Structure your growth experiments for maximum learning:

üéØ Hypothesis

Create a testable prediction about what will improve your performance

Example: "If I practice spaced repetition, I will retain 30% more information"

üìä Yardsticks

Define specific, measurable success criteria and tracking methods

Example: "Track recall accuracy on weekly tests for 4 weeks"

üî¨ Protocol

Design the specific steps and conditions of your experiment

Example: "Study for 25 minutes, review after 1 day, 3 days, 1 week"

‚è∞ Timeframe

Set clear start and end dates with evaluation milestones

Example: "4-week experiment with weekly progress assessments"

üìà Outcomes

Analyze results and extract insights for future experiments

Example: "Improved retention by 25%, will test optimal spacing intervals next"

üîÑ Scaling

Apply successful experiments broadly and design follow-up tests

Example: "Implement across all subjects, test with different content types"

üìö Integration

Build successful strategies into permanent systems and habits

Example: "Create spaced repetition app, teach method to study group"

üéØ Starting

Begin with small, low-risk experiments before major changes

Example: "Test on one subject before changing entire study system"

üìã Types of Growth Experiments

Different experimental approaches for different development goals:

üîÑ A/B Tests

Compare two different approaches over similar time periods

  • Morning vs. evening study sessions
  • Digital vs. handwritten note-taking
  • Solo vs. group practice methods

üìà Progressive Tests

Gradually increase intensity or complexity over time

  • Weekly meditation duration increases
  • Progressive physical challenge goals
  • Expanding comfort zone activities

üîÄ Sequential Tests

Try different approaches in succession to find optimal methods

  • Testing various learning techniques
  • Experimenting with different schedules
  • Trying alternative productivity systems

üìä Measurement and Analysis

Effective methods for tracking experimental outcomes:

  • Quantitative Metrics: Numbers that show objective progress and performance
  • Qualitative Observations: Subjective experiences, energy levels, and satisfaction ratings
  • Behavioral Indicators: Frequency, consistency, and quality of target behaviors
  • Outcome Measures: End results and achievement of specific goals
  • Process Measures: Quality and efficiency of methods used
  • Environmental Factors: External conditions that may influence results

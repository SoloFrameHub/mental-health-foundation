üåü Stafford & Canary's 5 Maintenance Strategies

Research-validated behaviors that predict relationship satisfaction and longevity:

STRATEGY 1: POSITIVITY (optimism, cheerfulness, avoiding unnecessary criticism)

‚Ä¢ What it is: Maintaining cheerful, optimistic interactions; focusing on positive traits; avoiding constant complaints/criticism

‚Ä¢ Why it matters: Positive interactions create pleasant atmosphere making people WANT to spend time together; criticism creates defensiveness/withdrawal

‚Ä¢ Examples: Complimenting friend on achievement, expressing appreciation for partner's efforts, maintaining humor even during stress, focusing on gratitude not grievances

‚Ä¢ Research: Gottman's 5:1 ratio‚Äîrelationships thrive when positive interactions outnumber negative 5 to 1 (criticism tolerable if balanced with positivity)

‚Ä¢ Caution: Positivity doesn't mean toxic positivity (suppressing real problems)‚Äîmeans addressing issues constructively without constant negativity

STRATEGY 2: OPENNESS (self-disclosure, meta-communication)

‚Ä¢ What it is: Sharing thoughts/feelings/experiences (self-disclosure) + talking about relationship itself (meta-communication: "how are WE doing?")

‚Ä¢ Why it matters: Openness creates intimacy and prevents relationship drift; meta-communication catches problems early before they escalate

‚Ä¢ Examples: Sharing vulnerable feelings with friend, discussing relationship concerns with partner ("I've felt distant lately‚Äîcan we talk?"), regular check-ins about relationship health

‚Ä¢ Research: Self-disclosure deepens relationships (Lesson 8.5 on vulnerability), but meta-communication MAINTAINS them by addressing issues proactively

‚Ä¢ Balance: Too little openness = superficial relationship; too much = overwhelming (gradual, reciprocal disclosure optimal)

STRATEGY 3: ASSURANCES (expressing commitment, importance, reliability)

‚Ä¢ What it is: Explicitly communicating commitment ("you're important to me"), reassuring relationship value, demonstrating reliability/consistency

‚Ä¢ Why it matters: Assurances combat insecurity and uncertainty; people need to know relationship matters and will continue

‚Ä¢ Examples: Telling friend "your friendship means the world to me," reassuring partner of commitment during stress, showing up consistently when promised

‚Ä¢ Research: Attachment theory‚Äîassurances create secure attachment by signaling "I'm here, I'm staying, you matter to me"

‚Ä¢ Frequency: Not just crisis moments‚Äîregular low-stakes assurances ("looking forward to our weekly call") build cumulative security

STRATEGY 4: SOCIAL NETWORKS (shared friends, integrating social circles)

‚Ä¢ What it is: Spending time with mutual friends, integrating into each other's social circles, supporting each other's friendships

‚Ä¢ Why it matters: Shared networks create "relationship infrastructure"‚Äîmutual friends reinforce bond; isolation weakens it

‚Ä¢ Examples: Couple hosting dinner with mutual friends, introducing close friend to other friends creating shared social circle, attending partner's work events, supporting friend's community involvement

‚Ä¢ Research: Relationships embedded in social networks more stable‚Äîmutual friends provide support, accountability, shared activities reinforcing connection

‚Ä¢ Balance: Some individual friendships healthy (autonomy), but NO shared networks = relationship isolated and vulnerable

STRATEGY 5: SHARING TASKS (equitable distribution of responsibilities)

‚Ä¢ What it is: Fair, equitable distribution of relationship maintenance work (planning, organizing, emotional labor, practical tasks)

‚Ä¢ Why it matters: Inequity breeds resentment; perceived fairness predicts satisfaction; feeling like you're doing all the work destroys goodwill

‚Ä¢ Examples: Taking turns planning friend hangouts (not always one person initiating), partners equally handling household/emotional labor, shared responsibility for maintaining family connection

‚Ä¢ Research: Equity theory‚Äîpeople assess relationship fairness by comparing inputs (effort) to outputs (benefits); perceived inequity = relationship dissatisfaction

‚Ä¢ Reality check: Perfect 50/50 impossible every moment‚Äîbut over TIME, both people should feel they're contributing fairly and being valued

APPLYING ALL 5: Research shows using 4+ strategies = 78% higher satisfaction. Relationships using 0-2 strategies = drift/decline. Not about perfection‚Äîabout INTENTIONAL, CONSISTENT application.

üéØ Strategic vs Routine Maintenance

Two types of relationship maintenance‚Äîboth essential for longevity:

ROUTINE MAINTENANCE (everyday habits):

‚Ä¢ What it is: Daily/weekly relationship habits‚Äîhow you normally interact, communicate, spend time together

‚Ä¢ Examples: Regular texting/calling, casual conversations, routine activities together (coffee, walks, watching shows), everyday courtesy/kindness

‚Ä¢ Purpose: Creates relationship baseline‚Äîsteady, predictable connection preventing neglect

‚Ä¢ Limitation: Routine alone ‚Üí stagnation, taking relationship for granted, lack of novelty/growth

‚Ä¢ Strength: Low-effort, sustainable‚Äîdoesn't require intense planning, just consistent showing up

STRATEGIC MAINTENANCE (intentional relationship-building):

‚Ä¢ What it is: Deliberate, planned activities specifically designed to strengthen relationship‚Äîrequires forethought and effort

‚Ä¢ Examples: Planning special birthday celebration, scheduling monthly "state of friendship" check-in, organizing weekend trip together, creating new shared experience/tradition

‚Ä¢ Purpose: Prevents relationship drift, creates novelty/excitement, deepens intimacy, addresses problems proactively

‚Ä¢ Limitation: Requires time/energy/planning‚Äîcan't sustain constant strategic maintenance (exhausting)

‚Ä¢ Strength: High-impact‚Äîcreates memorable experiences, relationship growth, course-correction when needed

OPTIMAL BALANCE:

‚Ä¢ Routine maintenance: 80-90% of relationship time (sustainable baseline keeping relationship alive)

‚Ä¢ Strategic maintenance: 10-20% of relationship time (intentional boosts preventing drift)

‚Ä¢ Example weekly balance (close friendship): Routine = daily texting, weekly coffee (routine). Strategic = monthly deeper check-in call, quarterly new experience together (strategic).

‚Ä¢ Example monthly balance (partnership): Routine = daily meals together, regular conversations (routine). Strategic = monthly date night with new activity, quarterly relationship review (strategic).

Warning: All routine, no strategic = relationship stagnation (taken for granted, drifting apart). All strategic, no routine = unsustainable (burnout, performative). Need BOTH.

üîÑ Quarterly Relationship Review System

WHEN: Every 3 months (quarterly)

‚Ä¢ Schedule in calendar as non-negotiable "relationship maintenance time"

‚Ä¢ For close friendships: quarterly phone/video call or coffee specifically for check-in
